\begin{abstract}
$\mu$Flow is an open-source, browser-based simulator that models the complete signal chain of microfluidic Hall-effect magnetic bead detection in an interactive 3D environment. The tool implements four coupled physics models (magnetic dipole stray-field computation, volume-averaged Hall voltage generation, Poiseuille flow transport, and Halbach permanent-magnet excitation) parameterized from published experimental data. Users can inject finite batches of superparamagnetic beads, observe their flow through a microchannel in real time via a Three.js 3D scene, and quantify the resulting Hall voltage pulses with per-bead statistics. Three pre-validated sensor designs spanning $5 \times 5$~$\mu$m to $50 \times 50$~$\mu$m are included, matching the finite-element benchmarks in Govindaraju and Hassan (2025). $\mu$Flow requires no installation, no server, and no license; the entire application is a single HTML file that runs client-side in any modern web browser.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation and significance}
% ========================================================================

Microfluidic Hall-effect sensors detect individual superparamagnetic beads by measuring the transient stray magnetic field as immunomagnetically labeled analytes pass over a thin-film Hall element~\cite{shah2023,issadore2014}. Hall sensors are linear over a wide dynamic range, can be miniaturized to match single-cell volumes, and operate in complex biofluids without optical instrumentation~\cite{collomb2021}. Besse et al.\ demonstrated single-bead detection with a 2.4~$\mu$m silicon Hall sensor producing a 5.5~$\mu$T signal from a 2.8~$\mu$m Dynabead~\cite{besse2002}. Mihajlovi\'{c} et al.\ achieved detection of individual 2.8~$\mu$m beads using a 1~$\mu$m InAs quantum-well sensor~\cite{mihajlovic2005}. More recently, Shah et al.\ integrated CVD graphene Hall sensors ($S_I = 175$~VA$^{-1}$T$^{-1}$) with a 50~$\mu$m PDMS microchannel and detected 47~$\mu$m ferrite-agarose beads in whole blood with SNR $> 20$~dB~\cite{shah2023}. Dauber et al.\ reported graphene-hBN Hall sensors with a minimum detectable field of 50~nT/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$, the lowest among room-temperature Hall technologies~\cite{dauber2015}.

Despite this progress, the design of microfluidic Hall biosensors remains challenging because the detected signal depends on the coupled interaction of at least four physics domains: (i)~the magnetization of the bead in the applied field, governed by its ferrite content and susceptibility; (ii)~the spatial distribution of the bead's dipolar stray field, which decays as $1/r^3$; (iii)~the Hall voltage produced by averaging this inhomogeneous field over the sensor active area; and (iv)~the hydrodynamic transport that determines the bead trajectory and transit time through the sensing zone. Commercial finite-element tools such as COMSOL Multiphysics can model each of these domains, but setting up a coupled magnetostatic--hydrodynamic simulation requires specialized expertise, a software license (typically \$3,000--\$8,000/year), and hours of computation per parameter sweep. Our own prior work used a COMSOL FEM-BEM model to compare four semiconductor platforms and three sensor geometries, requiring over 200 simulations~\cite{govindaraju2025}. No open-source, interactive alternative existed.

$\mu$Flow addresses this gap. It is, to our knowledge, the first publicly available simulator that integrates all four physics domains into a real-time, browser-based, 3D visualization. The tool is designed for three user communities: (1)~researchers exploring Hall sensor design trade-offs before committing to fabrication; (2)~educators teaching biosensor physics in courses where commercial FEM licenses are unavailable; and (3)~students learning the interplay between magnetic, electronic, and fluidic phenomena in lab-on-chip systems.

\subsection{Physics engine}
\label{sec:physics}

$\mu$Flow implements the analytical model from Govindaraju and Hassan~\cite{govindaraju2025}, which was validated against COMSOL FEM-BEM simulations across three sensor designs. The physics engine consists of four coupled modules.

\paragraph{Bead magnetization.}
Each superparamagnetic bead of radius $r_b$ and core relative permeability $\mu_r$ is modeled as a dielectric sphere in a uniform applied field $B_0$. The induced magnetic dipole moment is
\begin{equation}
m = \frac{4}{3}\pi r_b^3 \cdot \chi_{\text{eff}} \cdot \frac{B_0}{\mu_0},
\label{eq:moment}
\end{equation}
where $\chi_{\text{eff}} = 3(\mu_r - 1)/(\mu_r + 2)$ is the Clausius--Mossotti effective susceptibility. For magnetite with $\mu_r = 10$, this gives $\chi_{\text{eff}} = 2.25$. The model uses bulk susceptibility rather than a Langevin nanoparticle ensemble, as validated against experimental bead characterization data~\cite{fonnum2005,dalslet2008}.

\paragraph{Stray field.}
The axial component of the magnetic field from an induced point dipole displaced by $(\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta z)$ from a field point is~\cite{griffiths}
\begin{equation}
B_z = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot m \cdot \frac{2\Delta z^2 - \Delta x^2 - \Delta y^2}{(\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2 + \Delta z^2)^{5/2}}.
\label{eq:dipole}
\end{equation}
This point-dipole approximation is valid when the bead-to-sensor distance exceeds the bead radius, which holds for all three sensor designs.

\paragraph{Volume-averaged Hall voltage.}
The Hall voltage depends on the average $B_z$ over the sensor volume, not on the surface field alone. $\mu$Flow computes this by numerical quadrature over an $n_{xy} \times n_{xy} \times n_z$ grid spanning the sensor volume (width $w$, thickness $t$):
\begin{equation}
\langle B_z \rangle = \frac{1}{n_{xy}^2 \cdot n_z} \sum_{i,j,k} B_z(\Delta x_i, \Delta y_j, \Delta z_k),
\label{eq:avgbz}
\end{equation}
where each grid point samples Eq.~\eqref{eq:dipole}. The resulting Hall voltage is
\begin{equation}
\Delta V_H = R_H \cdot \sigma \cdot \langle B_z \rangle \cdot V_{\text{bias}},
\label{eq:vh}
\end{equation}
with Hall coefficient $R_H = 1.25 \times 10^{-3}$~m$^3$/C and conductivity $\sigma = 1040$~S/m, matching the highly doped N-type semiconductor sensor characterized in~\cite{govindaraju2025} (Table~1). Govindaraju and Hassan showed that $\Delta V_H$ scales linearly with $V_{\text{bias}}$ (slope $\approx 0.078$) across all three designs, and that nonmagnetic control particles ($\mu_r = 1$) produce no measurable signal.

\paragraph{Microfluidic transport.}
Bead trajectories are governed by Poiseuille flow in a rectangular microchannel. For a channel of height $H$, the velocity at vertical position $y$ is
\begin{equation}
v(y) = \frac{3}{2}\,\bar{v}\left[1 - \left(\frac{2y}{H} - 1\right)^2\right],
\label{eq:poiseuille}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{v}$ is the mean flow velocity. In fixed-height mode, all beads travel at the same height with uniform velocity for controlled comparison. In range mode, beads are distributed across the channel cross-section and experience the full parabolic profile plus optional magnetophoretic drift toward the sensor surface.

\subsection{Software architecture}

$\mu$Flow is a single HTML file ($\approx$1{,}000 lines) containing all markup, styling, and application logic. The technology stack comprises:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{React~18} (UMD via CDN) for reactive state management and UI rendering, using \texttt{createElement} calls (no JSX compilation required).
\item \textbf{Three.js~0.160} (ES module via CDN) for the 3D scene: microchannel geometry, CHMA permanent-magnet ring, animated bead spheres, and Hall sensor element.
\item \textbf{HTML5 Canvas} for the 2D signal trace panel showing real-time $\Delta V_H$ versus bead position.
\end{itemize}

This architecture eliminates all build tools, package managers, and server-side dependencies. The application is deployed as a static file on Vercel and can alternatively be opened from a local file system. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} shows the data-flow architecture.

\centering

\fbox{\parbox{0.9\columnwidth}{\centering\vspace{2cm}\textit{[Figure placeholder: data-flow architecture diagram]}\vspace{2cm}}}
\caption{Data-flow architecture of $\mu$Flow. React state drives physics computations via \texttt{useMemo}; results feed both the Three.js 3D scene and the HTML5 Canvas signal trace. The simulation loop uses adaptive sub-stepping to capture narrow signal peaks.}

The simulation loop runs inside \texttt{requestAnimationFrame} with adaptive sub-stepping. At each frame, the maximum bead displacement is compared to $0.4 \times w_{\text{sensor}}$; if the displacement exceeds this threshold, the frame is subdivided into $n_{\text{sub}}$ steps to ensure the narrow signal peak (typically $\sim$1 sensor width) is not missed. Peak $\Delta V_H$ is tracked within the sub-step loop, not at the frame boundary.

The user interface (Fig.~\ref{fig:screenshot}) provides the following capabilities:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Sensor design selection.} Three pre-configured designs from~\cite{govindaraju2025}:
  \begin{itemize}
  \item Design~I: $5 \times 5$~$\mu$m sensor, 2~$\mu$m bead
  \item Design~II: $10 \times 10$~$\mu$m sensor, 6~$\mu$m bead
  \item Design~III: $50 \times 50$~$\mu$m sensor, 10~$\mu$m bead
  \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Parameter sliders.} Real-time control of: applied field $B_0$ (10--500~mT), bead height $r$ above sensor, flow velocity $\bar{v}$ (0.1--20~mm/s), channel dimensions, and bias voltage.

\item \textbf{3D scene.} An interactive Three.js scene displaying the PDMS microchannel, Hall sensor element, superparamagnetic beads, and a circular Halbach magnetic array (CHMA) modeled after the 8-magnet N52~NdFeB ring ($B_r = 1.44$~T, inner diameter 30~mm) described in~\cite{govindaraju2025}. N/S pole labels and $B_0$ field-direction arrows are rendered on the array. Camera rotation, zoom, and pan via OrbitControls.

\item \textbf{Signal trace.} Real-time 2D plot of $\Delta V_H$ versus time as beads traverse the sensor, rendered on a Canvas element below the 3D viewport.

\item \textbf{Batch mode.} Inject a configurable number of beads ($N = 1$--50), let all pass through the channel, then view batch statistics: mean peak $\Delta V_H$, standard deviation, coefficient of variation (CV\%), detection efficiency, and a per-bead results table.

\item \textbf{Height modes.} ``Fixed height'' mode (uniform velocity, no drift) for controlled experiments; ``Range'' mode for realistic Poiseuille profile with height-dependent velocity.

\item \textbf{Paper reference values.} Each sensor preset displays the expected $\Delta V_H$ from~\cite{govindaraju2025} alongside the simulated value for immediate validation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Illustrative examples}
% ========================================================================

We demonstrate $\mu$Flow through two use cases that illustrate its utility for sensor design exploration and signal quantification.

\subsection{Example 1: Sensor design comparison}

A common early-stage design question is how sensor size affects the detected signal. Using $\mu$Flow, a user selects each of the three sensor presets in sequence and observes:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Design~I} ($5 \times 5$~$\mu$m, 2~$\mu$m bead, $B_0 = 50$~mT): $\Delta V_H \approx 20$~mV. The sensor is comparable in size to the bead, producing a strong average field. The 3D view shows the bead nearly filling the sensor cross-section.
\item \textbf{Design~II} ($10 \times 10$~$\mu$m, 6~$\mu$m bead, $B_0 = 50$~mT): $\Delta V_H \approx 85$~mV. This configuration produces the largest signal because the 6~$\mu$m bead generates a stronger dipole moment ($m \propto r_b^3$) while the sensor is still small enough to maintain a high average field.
\item \textbf{Design~III} ($50 \times 50$~$\mu$m, 10~$\mu$m bead, $B_0 = 50$~mT): $\Delta V_H \approx 3$~mV. Despite the largest bead, the signal is weakest because averaging over the large sensor area dilutes the localized stray field.
\end{itemize}

These values agree with the COMSOL FEM results in~\cite{govindaraju2025} to within a factor of~2, consistent with the known overestimate of the point-dipole approximation at close range.

\subsection{Example 2: Batch mode signal quantification}

To assess detection reliability, the user configures Design~II with 10 beads at fixed height ($r = 3.1$~$\mu$m), flow velocity 1~mm/s, and clicks ``Run Batch.'' After all beads pass, $\mu$Flow displays:

\begin{itemize}
\item Mean peak $\Delta V_H$: 85.2~mV
\item Standard deviation: 1.4~mV
\item CV: 1.7\%
\item Detection efficiency: 100\% (10/10 beads)
\end{itemize}

The low CV confirms that at fixed height, the signal variation arises solely from numerical discretization. Switching to range mode introduces height-dependent variation, increasing the CV to $\sim$15--30\% depending on the height distribution, reflecting the realistic scenario where beads at different heights produce different stray fields at the sensor.

\section{Impact}
% ========================================================================

$\mu$Flow addresses a practical gap in the microfluidic biosensor design workflow. Before $\mu$Flow, a researcher wanting to estimate the Hall voltage from a particular bead--sensor combination had two options: run a multi-hour COMSOL simulation (requiring a license and FEM expertise) or implement the analytical equations from scratch in MATLAB or Python. $\mu$Flow reduces this to a few seconds of slider adjustment in a web browser.

The tool is particularly suited for:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Rapid prototyping.} Before fabricating a new sensor geometry or selecting a bead vendor, a researcher can use $\mu$Flow to estimate whether the expected signal exceeds the noise floor of their readout electronics.
\item \textbf{Education.} The 3D visualization of the bead's stray field, the Halbach array's uniform applied field, and the Poiseuille flow profile provides an intuitive, spatial understanding of the sensing mechanism that equations alone do not convey. The tool can supplement biosensor or MEMS courses at institutions that lack COMSOL licenses.
\item \textbf{Reproducibility.} Because $\mu$Flow implements the exact analytical model from a peer-reviewed publication~\cite{govindaraju2025}, other groups can independently verify our published results or extend the model with their own sensor parameters.
\end{itemize}

The deployed application is accessible at \url{https://microfluidic-hall-sensor.vercel.app} and the source code is available under the MIT license at \url{https://github.com/hg293/microfluidic-hall-sensor}.

\section{Conclusions}
% ========================================================================

We have presented $\mu$Flow, a browser-based interactive simulator for microfluidic Hall-effect magnetic bead detection. The tool combines four physics modules (bead magnetization, dipolar stray field, volume-averaged Hall voltage, and Poiseuille flow) with a Three.js 3D visualization and batch-mode signal analysis, all within a single HTML file requiring no installation or license. The physics engine reproduces the analytical model validated against COMSOL FEM-BEM simulations in our prior work, enabling researchers to explore sensor design trade-offs in seconds rather than hours. Future extensions may include Langevin nanoparticle-ensemble magnetization, 1/f noise modeling, and multi-bead interference effects.
