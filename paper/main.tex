\begin{abstract}
$\mu$Flow is an open-source, browser-based simulator that models the complete signal chain of microfluidic Hall-effect magnetic bead detection in an interactive 3D environment. The tool implements four coupled physics models (magnetic dipole stray-field computation, volume-averaged Hall voltage generation, Poiseuille flow transport, and Halbach permanent-magnet excitation) parameterized from published experimental data. Users can inject finite batches of superparamagnetic beads, observe their flow through a microchannel in real time via a Three.js 3D scene, and quantify the resulting Hall voltage pulses with per-bead statistics. Three pre-validated sensor designs spanning $5 \times 5$~$\mu$m to $50 \times 50$~$\mu$m are included, matching the finite-element benchmarks in Govindaraju and Hassan (2025). $\mu$Flow requires no installation, no server, and no license; the entire application is a single HTML file that runs client-side in any modern web browser.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation and significance}
% ========================================================================

Microfluidic Hall-effect sensors detect individual superparamagnetic beads by measuring the transient stray magnetic field as labeled analytes pass over a thin-film Hall element. Ejsing et al.\ demonstrated this principle using a planar Hall effect (PHE) sensor that produced a 0.3~$\mu$V signal change from a single 2~$\mu$m bead magnetized at 20~mT~\cite{ejsing2004}. Shah et al.\ later integrated CVD graphene Hall sensors with current-related sensitivity $S_I = 175$~VA$^{-1}$T$^{-1}$ into a 50~$\mu$m PDMS microchannel and detected 47~$\mu$m ferrite-agarose beads in whole blood with SNR $> 20$~dB~\cite{shah2023}. Aledealat et al.\ showed that an InAs quantum-well micro-Hall sensor could track individual 2.8~$\mu$m Dynabeads flowing through a microfluidic channel in real time, observing sign-resolved voltage transients as beads entered and exited the Hall cross~\cite{aledealat2009}. Gabureac et al.\ pushed the size limit further with a 500~nm nanocomposite Hall sensor that detected a single 1~$\mu$m bead and traced its position across the sensor area~\cite{gabureac2013}.

Graphene is increasingly attractive as a Hall sensor material because its low carrier density and high mobility yield large Hall coefficients at room temperature. Dauber et al.\ fabricated graphene encapsulated in hexagonal boron nitride and achieved a minimum detectable field of 50~nT/$\sqrt{\text{Hz}}$ at room temperature, among the lowest for any Hall material~\cite{dauber2015}. Kaidarova et al.\ demonstrated a flexible Hall sensor from laser-scribed graphene on polyimide with a sensitivity of 0.097~V/VT that survived bending to a 4~mm radius~\cite{kaidarova2021}. Petit et al.\ showed through Boltzmann transport simulations that graphene Hall sensitivity depends on the interplay of bias current, charge disorder, and Fermi velocity, and that the two-channel model commonly used for analysis breaks down near the charge neutrality point~\cite{petit2024}.

Despite this progress, the design of microfluidic Hall biosensors remains challenging because the detected signal depends on the coupled interaction of at least four physics domains: (i)~the magnetization of the bead in the applied field, governed by its ferrite content and effective susceptibility; (ii)~the spatial distribution of the bead's dipolar stray field, which decays as $1/r^3$; (iii)~the Hall voltage produced by averaging this inhomogeneous field over the sensor active area; and (iv)~the hydrodynamic transport that determines the bead trajectory and transit time through the sensing zone. Commercial finite-element tools such as COMSOL Multiphysics can model each of these domains, but setting up a coupled magnetostatic-hydrodynamic simulation requires specialized expertise, a software license (typically \$3,000--\$8,000/year), and hours of computation per parameter sweep. Jebari et al.\ reported that their 3D multiphysics simulation of a microfluidic immunosensor with magnetic nanoparticle transport required COMSOL and coupled Navier--Stokes, convection-diffusion, and magnetostatic solvers across multiple geometry variants~\cite{jebari2024}. Our own prior work used a COMSOL FEM-BEM model to compare four semiconductor platforms and three sensor geometries, requiring over 200 simulations~\cite{govindaraju2025}. No open-source, interactive alternative existed.

$\mu$Flow addresses this gap. It is, to our knowledge, the first publicly available simulator that integrates all four physics domains into a real-time, browser-based, 3D visualization. The tool is designed for three user communities: (1)~researchers exploring Hall sensor design trade-offs before committing to fabrication; (2)~educators teaching biosensor physics in courses where commercial FEM licenses are unavailable; and (3)~students learning the interplay between magnetic, electronic, and fluidic phenomena in lab-on-chip systems. Lab-on-chip platforms are under active development for point-of-care diagnostics~\cite{tymm2020,coarsey2019,kabir2020}, and magnetic bead-based immunoassays in microfluidic channels have been demonstrated for both sandwich ELISA~\cite{bhalla2013} and pathogen detection~\cite{kabir2020}. A freely accessible simulation tool can accelerate sensor design iterations in these applications.

\subsection{Comparison with existing tools}

Table~\ref{tab:comparison} compares $\mu$Flow against the available alternatives. The MMFT simulator~\cite{emmerich2024} models microfluidic networks using a 1D hydraulic-circuit analogy but does not include magnetic sensing. The nanoHUB BioSensorLab~\cite{nanohub} models electrostatic biosensors using Poisson--Boltzmann equations, targeting FET-based detection rather than magnetic beads. Gurvich and Geller developed Firefly, a browser-based Three.js tool for interactive 3D visualization of particle datasets containing up to 10 million points~\cite{gurvich2022}; $\mu$Flow adopts a similar browser-native architecture but couples the 3D rendering with real-time physics computation. Stein et al.\ showed that browser-based simulations with block-based programming interfaces lower barriers to classroom adoption compared to installed software~\cite{stein2023}; $\mu$Flow extends this principle to graduate-level sensor physics. COMSOL and custom MATLAB/Python scripts can model individual physics modules but require programming expertise and are not interactive. $\mu$Flow is the only tool that combines 3D visualization, real-time parameter exploration, batch-mode signal quantification, and zero-install deployment in a single package.

\subsection{Physics engine}
\label{sec:physics}

$\mu$Flow implements the analytical model from Govindaraju and Hassan~\cite{govindaraju2025}, which was validated against COMSOL FEM-BEM simulations across three sensor designs. The physics engine consists of four coupled modules.

\paragraph{Bead magnetization.}
Each superparamagnetic bead of radius $r_b$ and core relative permeability $\mu_r$ is modeled as a dielectric sphere in a uniform applied field $B_0$. The induced magnetic dipole moment is
\begin{equation}
m = \frac{4}{3}\pi r_b^3 \cdot \chi_{\text{eff}} \cdot \frac{B_0}{\mu_0},
\label{eq:moment}
\end{equation}
where $\chi_{\text{eff}} = 3(\mu_r - 1)/(\mu_r + 2)$ is the Clausius--Mossotti effective susceptibility. For magnetite with $\mu_r = 10$, this gives $\chi_{\text{eff}} = 2.25$. The model uses bulk susceptibility rather than a Langevin nanoparticle ensemble. Elfimova et al.\ showed through Monte Carlo simulations that the Langevin function accurately describes the equilibrium magnetization of immobilized, weakly interacting superparamagnetic nanoparticles when inter-particle dipolar interactions are small~\cite{elfimova2019}. However, for composite beads containing many nanoparticle cores (e.g., Dynabeads), the Clausius--Mossotti formulation with an effective $\mu_r$ fitted to VSM data is more practical and reproduces bead-level magnetization curves without requiring knowledge of the internal nanoparticle size distribution~\cite{ota2019,sitbon2020}. Henrard et al.\ confirmed that Langevin-law fitting to DC magnetization curves can accurately recover the size distribution of superparamagnetic particles in the regime where thermal fluctuations dominate~\cite{henrard2019}.

\paragraph{Stray field.}
The axial component of the magnetic field from an induced point dipole displaced by $(\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta z)$ from a field point is~\cite{griffiths}
\begin{equation}
B_z = \frac{\mu_0}{4\pi} \cdot m \cdot \frac{2\Delta z^2 - \Delta x^2 - \Delta y^2}{(\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2 + \Delta z^2)^{5/2}}.
\label{eq:dipole}
\end{equation}
This point-dipole approximation is valid when the bead-to-sensor distance exceeds the bead radius, which holds for all three sensor designs. Jenkins et al.\ showed through atomistic dipole summation that stray fields from finite-volume magnetic sources converge to the point-dipole expression within 5\% when the observation distance exceeds 1.5 times the source radius~\cite{jenkins2019}. Manzin et al.\ used a similar dipolar stray-field model for simulating the interaction between superparamagnetic beads and magnonic crystal sensors, treating each bead as a uniformly magnetized sphere whose external field matches Eq.~\eqref{eq:dipole}~\cite{manzin2022}.

\paragraph{Volume-averaged Hall voltage.}
The Hall voltage depends on the average $B_z$ over the sensor volume, not on the surface field alone. $\mu$Flow computes this by numerical quadrature over an $n_{xy} \times n_{xy} \times n_z$ grid spanning the sensor volume (width $w$, thickness $t$):
\begin{equation}
\langle B_z \rangle = \frac{1}{n_{xy}^2 \cdot n_z} \sum_{i,j,k} B_z(\Delta x_i, \Delta y_j, \Delta z_k),
\label{eq:avgbz}
\end{equation}
where each grid point samples Eq.~\eqref{eq:dipole}. The resulting Hall voltage is
\begin{equation}
\Delta V_H = R_H \cdot \sigma \cdot \langle B_z \rangle \cdot V_{\text{bias}},
\label{eq:vh}
\end{equation}
with Hall coefficient $R_H = 1.25 \times 10^{-3}$~m$^3$/C and conductivity $\sigma = 1040$~S/m, matching the N-type semiconductor sensor characterized in~\cite{govindaraju2025} (Table~1). Tu et al.\ showed that the detected signal from a planar Hall sensor scales linearly with the sensor active area when the bead is centered, but drops sharply when the bead is offset laterally~\cite{tu2009}. This volume-averaging captures that spatial dependence. Uddin et al.\ further demonstrated through FEM optimization that elliptical sensor geometries can increase the effective sensing area by 15--20\% compared to square designs of the same footprint~\cite{uddin2022}.

\paragraph{Microfluidic transport.}
Bead trajectories are governed by Poiseuille flow in a rectangular microchannel. For a channel of height $H$, the velocity at vertical position $y$ is
\begin{equation}
v(y) = \frac{3}{2}\,\bar{v}\left[1 - \left(\frac{2y}{H} - 1\right)^2\right],
\label{eq:poiseuille}
\end{equation}
where $\bar{v}$ is the mean flow velocity. Hewlin and Edwards used this parabolic profile to model particle trajectories in a Y-shaped microfluidic channel for dielectrophoretic separation, confirming that laminar flow assumptions hold for Reynolds numbers below $\sim$1 in channels of comparable dimensions~\cite{hewlin2023}. Khashan et al.\ showed that coupling Poiseuille flow with magnetophoretic body forces enables selective sorting of labeled vs.\ unlabeled particles in similar microfluidic geometries~\cite{khashan2024}. In $\mu$Flow, fixed-height mode sets all beads to the same $y$ position with uniform velocity for controlled comparison. In range mode, beads are distributed across the channel cross-section and experience the full parabolic profile plus optional magnetophoretic drift toward the sensor surface.

\paragraph{Halbach array excitation.}
The applied field $B_0$ is generated by a cylindrical Halbach magnet array (CHMA) consisting of 8 N52~NdFeB segments with remanence $B_r = 1.44$~T and inner diameter 30~mm, as described in~\cite{govindaraju2025}. Klein et al.\ showed that Halbach configurations produce a more uniform field in the sample region than parallel-plate magnet pairs, with field homogeneity depending primarily on the ratio of magnet inner diameter to sample length~\cite{klein2021}. Descamps et al.\ demonstrated that self-assembled permanent micro-magnets in PDMS microfluidic devices can generate local field gradients exceeding 100~T/m for magnetophoretic cell sorting~\cite{descamps2021}. Li et al.\ derived analytical expressions for the air-gap field of trapezoidal Halbach arrays using an improved equivalent surface current method, confirming that the Halbach geometry concentrates flux on one side while canceling it on the other~\cite{li2023halbach}. In $\mu$Flow, the CHMA is rendered as a 3D ring in the Three.js scene with N/S pole labels and field-direction arrows.

\subsection{Software architecture}

$\mu$Flow is a single HTML file ($\approx$1{,}000 lines) containing all markup, styling, and application logic. The technology stack comprises:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{React~18} (UMD via CDN) for reactive state management and UI rendering, using \texttt{createElement} calls (no JSX compilation required).
\item \textbf{Three.js~0.160} (ES module via CDN) for the 3D scene: microchannel geometry, CHMA permanent-magnet ring, animated bead spheres, and Hall sensor element.
\item \textbf{HTML5 Canvas} for the 2D signal trace panel showing real-time $\Delta V_H$ versus bead position.
\end{itemize}

This architecture eliminates all build tools, package managers, and server-side dependencies. The application is deployed as a static file on Vercel and can alternatively be opened from a local file system. Gurvich and Geller adopted a similar CDN-based Three.js architecture for Firefly~\cite{gurvich2022}, demonstrating that browser-native scientific visualization tools can handle millions of rendered elements without a backend. Figure~\ref{fig:architecture} shows the data-flow architecture.

\begin{figure}[h!]
\centering
% \includegraphics[width=\columnwidth]{figures/architecture.pdf}
\fbox{\parbox{0.9\columnwidth}{\centering\vspace{2cm}\textit{[Figure placeholder: data-flow architecture diagram]}\vspace{2cm}}}
\caption{Data-flow architecture of $\mu$Flow. React state drives physics computations via \texttt{useMemo}; results feed both the Three.js 3D scene and the HTML5 Canvas signal trace. The simulation loop uses adaptive sub-stepping to capture narrow signal peaks.}

The simulation loop runs inside \texttt{requestAnimationFrame} with adaptive sub-stepping. At each frame, the maximum bead displacement is compared to $0.4 \times w_{\text{sensor}}$; if the displacement exceeds this threshold, the frame is subdivided into $n_{\text{sub}}$ steps to ensure the narrow signal peak (typically $\sim$1 sensor width) is not missed. Peak $\Delta V_H$ is tracked within the sub-step loop, not at the frame boundary.

The user interface (Fig.~\ref{fig:screenshot}) provides the following capabilities:

\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Sensor design selection.} Three pre-configured designs from~\cite{govindaraju2025}:
  \begin{itemize}
  \item Design~I: $5 \times 5$~$\mu$m sensor, 2~$\mu$m bead
  \item Design~II: $10 \times 10$~$\mu$m sensor, 6~$\mu$m bead
  \item Design~III: $50 \times 50$~$\mu$m sensor, 10~$\mu$m bead
  \end{itemize}

\item \textbf{Parameter sliders.} Real-time control of: applied field $B_0$ (10--500~mT), bead height $r$ above sensor, flow velocity $\bar{v}$ (0.1--20~mm/s), channel dimensions, and bias voltage.

\item \textbf{3D scene.} An interactive Three.js scene displaying the PDMS microchannel, Hall sensor element, superparamagnetic beads, and a circular Halbach magnetic array (CHMA) modeled after the 8-magnet N52~NdFeB ring ($B_r = 1.44$~T, inner diameter 30~mm) described in~\cite{govindaraju2025}. N/S pole labels and $B_0$ field-direction arrows are rendered on the array. Camera rotation, zoom, and pan via OrbitControls.

\item \textbf{Signal trace.} Real-time 2D plot of $\Delta V_H$ versus time as beads traverse the sensor, rendered on a Canvas element below the 3D viewport. Dalslet et al.\ observed similar sign-resolved voltage transients when beads passed over magnetoresistive sensors in a microfluidic system, with the pulse shape depending on bead height and lateral offset~\cite{dalslet2008}.

\item \textbf{Batch mode.} Inject a configurable number of beads ($N = 1$--50), let all pass through the channel, then view batch statistics: mean peak $\Delta V_H$, standard deviation, coefficient of variation (CV\%), detection efficiency, and a per-bead results table.

\item \textbf{Height modes.} ``Fixed height'' mode (uniform velocity, no drift) for controlled experiments; ``Range'' mode for realistic Poiseuille profile with height-dependent velocity.

\item \textbf{Paper reference values.} Each sensor preset displays the expected $\Delta V_H$ from~\cite{govindaraju2025} alongside the simulated value for immediate validation.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Illustrative examples}
% ========================================================================

We demonstrate $\mu$Flow through two use cases that illustrate its utility for sensor design exploration and signal quantification.

\subsection{Example 1: Sensor design comparison}

A common early-stage design question is how sensor size affects the detected signal. Using $\mu$Flow, a user selects each of the three sensor presets in sequence and observes:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Design~I} ($5 \times 5$~$\mu$m, 2~$\mu$m bead, $B_0 = 50$~mT): $\Delta V_H \approx 20$~mV. The sensor is comparable in size to the bead, producing a strong average field. The 3D view shows the bead nearly filling the sensor cross-section.
\item \textbf{Design~II} ($10 \times 10$~$\mu$m, 6~$\mu$m bead, $B_0 = 50$~mT): $\Delta V_H \approx 85$~mV. This configuration produces the largest signal because the 6~$\mu$m bead generates a stronger dipole moment ($m \propto r_b^3$) while the sensor is still small enough to maintain a high average field.
\item \textbf{Design~III} ($50 \times 50$~$\mu$m, 10~$\mu$m bead, $B_0 = 50$~mT): $\Delta V_H \approx 3$~mV. Despite the largest bead, the signal is weakest because averaging over the large sensor area dilutes the localized stray field.
\end{itemize}

These values agree with the COMSOL FEM results in~\cite{govindaraju2025} to within a factor of~2, consistent with the known overestimate of the point-dipole approximation at close range. Kim et al.\ reported a comparable effect for their PHE sensor: when the sensor active area exceeded the bead's stray-field footprint, the volume-averaged signal decreased despite increasing bead moment~\cite{kim2014}. The competing methods literature confirms that this size-matching requirement applies equally to GMR-based detectors. Liang et al.\ achieved single-nanoparticle sensitivity with a spin-valve GMR sensor array by matching the sensor stripe width ($\sim$1~$\mu$m) to the stray-field decay length of 16~nm Fe$_3$O$_4$ nanoparticles~\cite{liang2017}. Ger et al.\ detected magnetic nanoparticles at concentrations as low as 1.95~$\mu$g/mL using a GMR sensor integrated with a PDMS microfluidic channel~\cite{ger2023}. Melnikov et al.\ used magnetoimpedance (MI) sensors based on [FeNi/Cu]$_5$ multilayers to detect stray fields from magnetic particles flowing through a cylindrical channel mimicking a blood vessel~\cite{melnikov2021}. Dowling et al.\ demonstrated resonance-based detection of 130~nm magnetic nanoparticle clusters using ferromagnetic antidot nanostructures in a microfluidic configuration~\cite{dowling2023}.

\subsection{Example 2: Batch mode signal quantification}

To assess detection reliability, the user configures Design~II with 10 beads at fixed height ($r = 3.1$~$\mu$m), flow velocity 1~mm/s, and clicks ``Run Batch.'' After all beads pass, $\mu$Flow displays:

\begin{itemize}
\item Mean peak $\Delta V_H$: 85.2~mV
\item Standard deviation: 1.4~mV
\item CV: 1.7\%
\item Detection efficiency: 100\% (10/10 beads)
\end{itemize}

The low CV confirms that at fixed height, the signal variation arises solely from numerical discretization. Switching to range mode introduces height-dependent variation, increasing the CV to $\sim$15--30\% depending on the height distribution, reflecting the realistic scenario where beads at different heights produce different stray fields at the sensor. This height sensitivity is well documented: Aledealat et al.\ observed that the Hall voltage transient amplitude from individual 2.8~$\mu$m Dynabeads varied by more than a factor of 2 depending on the bead's vertical position in the microfluidic channel~\cite{aledealat2009}.

\section{Impact}
% ========================================================================

$\mu$Flow addresses a practical gap in the microfluidic biosensor design workflow. Before $\mu$Flow, a researcher wanting to estimate the Hall voltage from a particular bead-sensor combination had two options: run a multi-hour COMSOL simulation (requiring a license and FEM expertise) or implement the analytical equations from scratch in MATLAB or Python. $\mu$Flow reduces this to a few seconds of slider adjustment in a web browser.

The tool is suited for:

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{Rapid prototyping.} Before fabricating a new sensor geometry or selecting a bead vendor, a researcher can use $\mu$Flow to estimate whether the expected signal exceeds the noise floor of their readout electronics.
\item \textbf{Education.} The 3D visualization of the bead's stray field, the Halbach array's uniform applied field, and the Poiseuille flow profile provides spatial understanding of the sensing mechanism that equations alone do not convey. Stein et al.\ found that browser-based simulators with visual feedback increased student engagement in computational thinking tasks compared to text-only environments~\cite{stein2023}. The tool can supplement biosensor or MEMS courses at institutions that lack COMSOL licenses.
\item \textbf{Reproducibility.} Because $\mu$Flow implements the exact analytical model from a peer-reviewed publication~\cite{govindaraju2025}, other groups can independently verify our published results or extend the model with their own sensor parameters.
\end{itemize}

The deployed application is accessible at \url{https://microfluidic-hall-sensor.vercel.app} and the source code is available under the MIT license at \url{https://github.com/hg293/microfluidic-hall-sensor}.

\section{Conclusions}
% ========================================================================

We have presented $\mu$Flow, a browser-based interactive simulator for microfluidic Hall-effect magnetic bead detection. The tool combines four physics modules (bead magnetization, dipolar stray field, volume-averaged Hall voltage, and Poiseuille flow) with a Three.js 3D visualization and batch-mode signal analysis, all within a single HTML file requiring no installation or license. The physics engine reproduces the analytical model validated against COMSOL FEM-BEM simulations in our prior work~\cite{govindaraju2025}, enabling researchers to explore sensor design trade-offs in seconds rather than hours. Future extensions may include Langevin nanoparticle-ensemble magnetization for capturing saturation effects at high fields, 1/f noise modeling for signal-to-noise ratio estimation, and multi-bead interference effects for high-concentration assays.
